# Mental Models for Angle Generation

**Purpose:** Angle generation recipes for non-obvious content angles. Applied by the ideation agent before drafting begins.
**Invoked by:** content-ideator (Phase 5)
**Scope:** Angle generation ONLY — what to write about (a point of view, a premise). Article structure belongs in virality-patterns.md.

---

## Lenses

<!-- Lenses are observational frames — apply them to a subject to surface what is non-obvious -->

### Consensus Gap
**What it surfaces:** The gap between what experts believe and what practitioners widely accept as true
**Apply when:** The subject has a large body of conventional wisdom or established "best practice" advice

### Embedded Assumption
**What it surfaces:** Unstated premises the subject depends on being true — the hidden load-bearing beliefs inside common advice
**Apply when:** Conventional advice feels stale, produces mediocre outcomes, or only works for some people

### Second-Order Effects
**What it surfaces:** What happens after the obvious first consequence — the downstream consequences that follow from widely known consequences
**Apply when:** The subject is commonly framed as "do X → get Y" and Y is treated as the endpoint

### Contrarian Signal
**What it surfaces:** Claims that feel wrong or contrarian but are well-supported by data, logic, or accumulating evidence
**Apply when:** There is a strong prevailing consensus and quiet dissent from credible practitioners

### Underdog Evidence
**What it surfaces:** Data and examples that support the minority position — what the losing side got right
**Apply when:** A debate has a clear winner, but the losing side has evidence that never gets discussed

### Temporal Displacement
**What it surfaces:** Ideas that were true 5 years ago but are not now (or ideas from 20 years ago that are newly relevant again)
**Apply when:** The subject has a long history, has recently changed, or is experiencing a reversal

### Domain Transfer
**What it surfaces:** Insight from an unrelated field that applies to the subject in non-obvious ways — mechanisms that transfer across context
**Apply when:** The subject is mature and well-covered within its own domain but underexplored through outside lenses

### Granularity Mismatch
**What it surfaces:** Advice that works at scale but fails for individuals — or vice versa; the scope at which a claim is actually true
**Apply when:** Advice is given universally but you suspect it only applies at a specific scale, size, or stage

### Definitional Drift
**What it surfaces:** A commonly used term that means different things to different audiences — creating false consensus around a contested concept
**Apply when:** Everyone seems to agree on something but arguments keep breaking out anyway

### Missing Precondition
**What it surfaces:** The unstated prerequisite most readers don't have — advice that quietly assumes a condition the audience cannot rely on
**Apply when:** Advice is technically correct but produces poor results for most readers despite their sincere efforts

### Reversal of Causality
**What it surfaces:** Cases where the assumed cause and effect are backwards — where the effect produces the cause rather than the other way around
**Apply when:** The subject follows a "do X to get Y" formula that feels circular or self-referential

## Operations

<!-- Operations are thinking procedures — transforms applied to what lenses surface -->

### Invert
**Procedure:** Take the mainstream claim. State its opposite. Gather evidence, examples, and logical arguments that support the inversion. Stress-test: is the inverted claim defensible? If yes, find the narrowest version that is maximally defensible.
**Output:** A contrarian claim with supporting evidence and a precise scope of validity

### Specify
**Procedure:** Take a vague or universal claim. Ask: for whom is this true? Under what conditions? At what scale? What must already be true? Narrow the claim until it is as specific as possible while remaining true.
**Output:** A narrowed claim that is more true and more surprising than the original

### Historicize
**Procedure:** Ask when the current consensus was created. What event, data set, or cultural moment produced it? Who had incentives to establish it? What evidence existed at the time versus what we know now?
**Output:** A dated origin for a timeless-seeming belief — which destabilizes the belief

### Personalize
**Procedure:** Translate a systemic, aggregate, or statistical observation into a specific person's lived experience. Name the specific moment, decision, or feeling that the systemic pattern produces at the individual level.
**Output:** A human-scale angle on an abstract claim

### Propagate
**Procedure:** Start with a known consequence of X. Ask what that consequence causes. Then ask what that second consequence causes. Continue for 3–4 levels. Identify the level where no one is writing.
**Output:** A downstream consequence that exists but is not yet in the conversation

### Triangulate
**Procedure:** Find where two or more unrelated fields, disciplines, or time periods arrive at the same insight through different paths. Identify the shared underlying principle they are all pointing at.
**Output:** A convergent claim that gains credibility from being independently discovered multiple times

## Recipes

<!-- Recipes are named sequences that chain lens + operations to produce a specific type of non-obvious angle -->

### Recipe: Contrarian Angle Generator
**Problem:** Subject has strong conventional wisdom and you need an angle that challenges it without being wrong
**Steps:**
1. Apply Contrarian Signal lens to the subject — identify claims that feel wrong but have quiet evidence
2. Run Invert on the mainstream claim to make the contrast explicit
3. Run Specify to narrow the inverted claim to its most defensible scope
**Output:** The angle is: the conventional wisdom is correct at scale but wrong for [specific audience/condition/stage] — here is the evidence

### Recipe: Consensus Gap Exploiter
**Problem:** You sense a gap between what experts say and what practitioners do, but cannot articulate why it matters
**Steps:**
1. Apply Consensus Gap lens — name the specific claim where expert belief and practitioner behavior diverge
2. Run Historicize on the expert consensus to understand when and why it was established
3. Run Propagate to trace what maintaining the gap costs practitioners who follow expert advice
**Output:** The angle is: the expert advice was correct when it was established but the conditions that made it correct have changed — practitioners ignoring it are not wrong, they are ahead

### Recipe: Buried Assumption Excavator
**Problem:** Common advice produces mediocre results for most readers; you want to explain why without blaming the reader
**Steps:**
1. Apply Embedded Assumption lens — surface the unstated premise the advice depends on
2. Run Invert on the assumption — argue that the opposite is often true for the target reader
3. Find evidence that the inverted assumption matches many readers' actual conditions
**Output:** The angle is: the advice works perfectly — but only if you already have [X], which most readers do not; here is what to do if you do not have [X]

### Recipe: Domain Transfer Angle
**Problem:** Subject is thoroughly covered within its own domain and surface-level takes are exhausted
**Steps:**
1. Apply Domain Transfer lens — identify a different field with a solved version of the same problem
2. Run Specify on the exact mechanism that transfers — not the metaphor but the actual structural parallel
3. Run Personalize to translate the mechanism into the reader's specific context
**Output:** The angle is: [field X] solved this problem decades ago using [mechanism] — here is the precise way that mechanism applies to [subject] and what it implies for how you should act

### Recipe: Temporal Flip
**Problem:** Subject feels like old news but you sense the conversation has moved and most readers have not noticed
**Steps:**
1. Apply Temporal Displacement lens — identify when the current consensus was formed and what has changed since
2. Run Historicize to date the consensus precisely and name the event or data that created it
3. Run Propagate forward from the change to identify what the new reality implies 2–3 levels out
**Output:** The angle is: the [year] consensus on [subject] was correct for [condition] — that condition inverted in [year/event] and nobody updated the advice; here is what the update implies

### Recipe: Second-Order Reveal
**Problem:** Subject has obvious implications everyone is discussing; you need a take that goes further
**Steps:**
1. Apply Second-Order Effects lens — map the first-order consequences that are already widely known
2. Run Propagate 3 levels deep past what is commonly written about
3. Identify the level at which the consequence is surprising and not yet in the conversation
**Output:** The angle is: everyone is focused on [first-order effect], but [third-order effect] is the one that will matter most — here is why nobody is writing about it yet

### Recipe: Granularity Arbitrage
**Problem:** Universal advice is being applied everywhere, but you suspect it only works at specific scale or stage
**Steps:**
1. Apply Granularity Mismatch lens — identify the scope at which the common advice is actually true
2. Run Specify to define the exact condition (scale, size, stage, experience level) where the advice holds
3. Build the angle from the breakdown: what should readers outside that scope do instead?
**Output:** The angle is: [common advice] is correct for [specific scope] but actively harmful for [other scope] — most of the people reading advice about this are in the [other scope]

### Recipe: Definitional Guerrilla
**Problem:** Debate in the subject keeps going in circles; people seem to agree but keep arguing
**Steps:**
1. Apply Definitional Drift lens — identify the term at the center of the debate and surface its multiple meanings
2. Show two common uses of the term producing opposite conclusions when applied to the same situation
3. Run Invert — take a side: argue that one definition is the correct one for the reader's context and the other is producing noise
**Output:** The angle is: the endless debate about [X] exists because people are using the same word to mean two different things — once you define [X] as [definition], the debate resolves and [counterintuitive conclusion] follows

### Recipe: Missing Precondition Exposer
**Problem:** Advice is technically correct but produces poor results for most readers; you want to make readers feel seen rather than blamed
**Steps:**
1. Apply Missing Precondition lens — identify the unstated requirement the advice assumes is in place
2. Run Specify to describe exactly who has the precondition and who does not
3. Run Personalize to describe the experience of following correct advice without the precondition
**Output:** The angle is: the advice works — but only after [precondition] is true; most readers are trying to skip to step 3 when they are actually at step 0 — here is what step 0 actually looks like

### Recipe: Underdog Rehabilitation
**Problem:** Conventional wisdom has declared a clear winner in a debate, but you have evidence the losing side was right about something important
**Steps:**
1. Apply Underdog Evidence lens — find the specific claim the losing side made that is supported by evidence
2. Run Triangulate with one unrelated field that independently arrived at the same conclusion
3. Run Propagate to trace what it means for current practice if the underdog was right on that specific point
**Output:** The angle is: [conventional winner] is correct on [main question] but [underdog] was right about [specific mechanism] — and that mechanism matters more than anyone acknowledged; here is what changes if you take it seriously

### Recipe: Causal Reversal
**Problem:** Subject follows a "do X to get Y" formula that is producing confusing results in practice
**Steps:**
1. Apply Reversal of Causality lens — ask whether Y actually produces X rather than X producing Y
2. Run Historicize to identify where the X-causes-Y assumption came from and what evidence was used
3. Run Propagate to trace what changes about the recommended action if causality is reversed
**Output:** The angle is: everyone is told to do [X] to get [Y], but the relationship runs the other way — people who have [Y] naturally do [X]; the intervention is working backwards for most readers
