# Virality Patterns

**Purpose:** Hook templates, structure archetypes, and narrative patterns for Substack articles and LinkedIn posts.
**Invoked by:** content-curator (Phase 7 — hook analysis and structural critique), content-linkedin (Phase 8 — post generation)
**Platform scope:** Substack (email-based long-form) and LinkedIn (feed-based short-form). One file, both platforms.

---

## Substack Patterns

_Substack hooks are read in email preview: subject line + first ~100 characters of body.
The hook must earn an inbox click from someone scanning at speed. The opening line often
doubles as the email subject line._

### Substack Hook Patterns

### Hook: Provocative Inversion
**Form:** State the conventional wisdom as fact, then immediately contradict it — no hedge, no qualification.
**Template:** "Everyone says [X]. They're wrong. Here's what's actually happening."
**Constraint:** The inversion must land within the first ~100 characters. "Everyone says X. They're wrong." is 40 characters — budget the rest for the specific hook.
**When to use:** Subject has a dominant consensus that practitioners secretly doubt. Most effective when the inversion is your genuine position, not a rhetorical device.

### Hook: Specific Anomaly
**Form:** Name a single verifiable fact or observation that violates the expected pattern — then leave the explanation for the article body.
**Template:** "[Specific verifiable fact] — and nobody is talking about why."
**Constraint:** The fact must be concrete enough to be checked but surprising enough that the reader's first instinct is "wait, is that true?" The specificity is the credibility — vague anomalies don't earn the click.
**When to use:** You have access to data, a personal observation, or a documented case study that reveals something counterintuitive. Weak when the anomaly is well-known.

### Hook: Reader Accusation
**Form:** Tell readers they've been doing something wrong — but immediately shift blame to the advice itself, not to them.
**Template:** "You've been [doing X] wrong. Not because you're lazy. Because the advice is broken."
**Constraint:** The accusation must land in the email preview before the exculpation. Subject line often carries the accusation; first body line carries the reframe. The reversal must feel generous, not manipulative.
**When to use:** Subject involves widely followed advice that produces mediocre outcomes for most people who follow it correctly. Fails when the reader doesn't identify with [doing X].

### Hook: The Uncomfortable Question
**Form:** Ask a question the reader has subconsciously avoided — one that implies the answer is unflattering or disorienting.
**Template:** "Why does [common practice] produce [bad outcome] for most people who try it?"
**Constraint:** The question must implicate the reader's own experience in the email preview. Questions that feel distant or academic don't earn clicks — questions that feel like they're about the reader's specific situation do.
**When to use:** Topic where there's a gap between what people believe (or hope) and what typically happens. Works best when you have a real answer, not just the question.

### Hook: The Reframe Tease
**Form:** Name the surface topic the reader expects, then reveal a deeper or more surprising frame — all in the preview window.
**Template:** "This isn't really about [surface topic]. It's about [deeper thing]."
**Constraint:** Both the surface topic and the deeper thing must be legible in ~100 characters. The deeper thing must feel genuinely unexpected, not just a thematic generalization.
**When to use:** You've written about a specific topic but the real insight is one level of abstraction up. Strong for subjects where the reader thinks they know what the article is — and discovers they were wrong.

### Substack Structure Archetypes

### Structure: Deep Specific Insight
**Shape:** Earn credibility with a specific hook → build context (why this matters now) → deliver the counterintuitive core claim → support with evidence or examples → extend to implications → reframe what the reader thought they knew → close with a clear action or shift in belief.
**Best for:** Topics where the main value is a single non-obvious insight. The article exists to install one precise idea in the reader's mind.
**Section sequence:**
1. Hook (the anomaly or inversion)
2. Setup (why this question matters)
3. The counterintuitive claim (the core)
4. Evidence / examples (2–3 specific illustrations)
5. Implications (what changes if this is true)
6. Reframe (how to see the original topic differently)
7. Call to action or closing invitation
**Avoid:** Burying the core claim in section 4 or later. Long setup that exhausts the reader before the payoff. Multiple competing core claims.

### Structure: The Argument
**Shape:** Open with a clear position → steelman the best counterargument (genuinely, not as a strawman) → refute the steelman with the strongest evidence available → extend the argument to implications → close with a direct ask.
**Best for:** Topics where the writer has a minority or contested position they want to argue for persuasively. Readers who came expecting confirmation of their view should finish with a reason to update.
**Section sequence:**
1. The position (stated plainly, no throat-clearing)
2. Steelman (the strongest version of the opposing view)
3. The refutation (evidence, reasoning, or a framing that defeats the steelman)
4. Implications (if this argument is right, what follows?)
5. The ask (do something, change a belief, subscribe, share)
**Avoid:** Strawman counterarguments that readers can immediately identify as weak. Hedging the position after stating it. Closing without a direction for the reader to move.

### Structure: The Case Study
**Shape:** Open with a specific example → describe what most observers notice about it → reveal what most observers miss → extract the general principle the example illustrates → close by showing readers how to apply the principle.
**Best for:** Abstract principles that only become credible when grounded in one detailed, real example. Particularly strong when the example is counterintuitive or unfamiliar to the reader.
**Section sequence:**
1. The example (specific, named, situated in time and context)
2. The surface reading (what most people conclude from it)
3. The hidden layer (what the surface reading misses)
4. The general principle (what this example proves about a broader truth)
5. Application (how to use this principle in the reader's own context)
**Avoid:** Examples that are so famous the reader already has a fixed interpretation. Missing the transition from specific to general — case studies that never arrive at the principle are just stories.

### Structure: The Mental Model Introduction
**Shape:** Name and define a new concept → show it explaining three distinct situations the reader recognizes → give readers the concept as a tool they can carry.
**Best for:** Introducing a named framework or lens that reorganizes how readers think about a familiar domain. The article's value is the concept itself, not the supporting evidence.
**Section sequence:**
1. Name and define the concept (crisp, one or two sentences)
2. First application (familiar situation, reinterpreted through the lens)
3. Second application (different domain, same lens)
4. Third application (the most counterintuitive or surprising use)
5. The tool (how to apply this concept going forward — a simple prompt or test)
**Avoid:** Defining the concept so broadly it explains everything (and therefore nothing). More than three applications — the model should feel precise, not universal. Skipping the "how to use it" closing.

## LinkedIn Patterns

_LinkedIn hooks are constrained by the "see more" fold: ~150 characters on the first line.
Line 1 must stop the scroll on its own. No outbound links in body copy (link in first comment).
Short paragraphs, white space, no raw formatting._

### LinkedIn Hook Patterns

### Hook: Pattern Interrupt
**Form:** Open with something structurally unexpected — a number, a short question, a counter-statement — that breaks the visual rhythm of the feed before the reader consciously decides to read.
**Template:** "[Unexpected number or fragment]. [Short clarifying line that creates a gap.]"
**Constraint:** Line 1 must work as a complete stop-the-scroll unit within ~150 characters. The interruption is formal (how the line looks) and semantic (what the line implies). On LinkedIn the hook competes with career updates, job announcements, and thought-leader posts — generic professional advice is invisible.
**When to use:** When you have a specific data point, an unusual framing, or a claim that reads differently from everything else in the feed. Weak when the "interruption" is just a question that any post could open with.

### Hook: Bold Claim
**Form:** Make a single unqualified claim that most LinkedIn posts would soften, hedge, or walk back. The boldness earns the scroll-stop; the post earns the credibility.
**Template:** "[Unqualified claim that contradicts professional common sense]."
**Constraint:** The claim must be complete and unambiguous within the first line (~150 chars). Unlike Substack where a bold opening can be softened in the preview, LinkedIn's fold cuts exactly at line 1 — there is no second sentence to qualify. The claim stands alone or fails.
**When to use:** You have a position you genuinely hold that contradicts widely repeated LinkedIn wisdom. Fails when the "bold claim" is something most professionals already privately agree with (that's not bold, it's validation).

### Hook: Counter-Narrative
**Form:** Name a well-known LinkedIn belief, frame, or consensus — then directly challenge it, also within line 1.
**Template:** "Everyone on LinkedIn says [X]. That's how you stay mediocre."
**Constraint:** The consensus belief being challenged must be recognizable to the reader as a real LinkedIn trope — not a strawman of what people believe. LinkedIn's feed is saturated with counter-narratives; the specific target must be credible and the challenge must be sharper than "well, it depends."
**When to use:** You've observed a widespread piece of career or professional advice that produces predictably bad outcomes. Most effective when the belief is actively promoted by other LinkedIn influencers, not just generally held.

### Hook: The Confession
**Form:** Open with a first-person admission of a past wrong belief, past failure, or past behavior — without hedging or softening it.
**Template:** "I used to believe [X]. I was wrong."
**Constraint:** The confession must be specific enough to be credible and general enough for the reader to recognize in themselves — within line 1. A confession that sounds like a staged vulnerability exercise (too polished, too tidy) fails. The admission must cost something.
**When to use:** You've genuinely changed your mind on something professionally relevant, and the change point is specific and illustratable. Fails when the "wrong belief" is obviously wrong to everyone — there's no credibility in confessing to a belief no one actually holds.

### Hook: The Counterintuitive Lesson
**Form:** Name the expected thing, then name the actual thing that produces the result — creating cognitive dissonance that requires the post to resolve.
**Template:** "The [expected thing] isn't what [produces result]. It's [unexpected thing]."
**Constraint:** Both the expected thing and the unexpected thing must be named concretely within ~150 characters. On LinkedIn, "counterintuitive" is overused as a label — the actual content must demonstrate the inversion through specifics, not claim it. Vague inversions ("it's not about the strategy, it's about the mindset") have no stopping power.
**When to use:** You have a specific, documented observation about what actually drives an outcome that most professionals attribute to something else. Strongest when the unexpected thing is something the reader can immediately verify against their own experience.

### LinkedIn Structure Archetypes

### Structure: Insight + Proof + Implication
**Shape:** Open with a bold claim (the insight) → support with one specific, concrete example (the proof) → close with the direct consequence for the reader (the implication).
**Best for:** Single-idea posts where the value is one precise observation with clear relevance to the reader's professional life. The discipline is in narrowing to one claim, one example, one implication.
**Section sequence:**
1. Bold opening claim (1–2 lines max)
2. One specific example or data point that proves it (3–5 lines)
3. What this means for the reader (2–3 lines)
4. Optional: close with a question to drive comments
**Avoid:** Multiple claims competing for the same post. Abstract examples that don't feel lived-in. Implications that are generic ("this is why mindset matters") rather than specific ("if you're doing X, stop and do Y instead").

### Structure: List with a Twist
**Shape:** Open with a numbered or bulleted list of items that share an apparent pattern — then land a final item that inverts, subverts, or undermines the pattern the reader was building.
**Best for:** Teaching a nuanced point where the list format earns easy scanning but the final item earns the deeper takeaway. The format is familiar; the subversion earns engagement.
**Section sequence:**
1. Hook line naming the list (e.g., "5 things that actually grow a newsletter:")
2. Items 1–4: genuine, useful, unsurprising — build the reader's trust
3. Item 5 (or final item): the twist — inverts the implicit logic of the previous items
4. 1–2 line close that names the lesson the twist reveals
**Avoid:** A twist item that feels like a gotcha rather than a genuine reframe. Lists so long the reader loses the thread before the twist. A twist that's just "the real answer is simpler than all of these" — that's not a twist, it's a dismissal.

### Structure: Before/After
**Shape:** Describe the problem-state most readers are in (Before) → name the specific reframe or shift that changes everything (the turn) → describe the After state → point toward the full piece for depth.
**Best for:** Situations where the value proposition is a clear upgrade: from one way of thinking to a better way. Works when the Before state is one the reader recognizes and the After state is genuinely desirable.
**Section sequence:**
1. Before: describe the problem state in the reader's own terms (1–3 lines)
2. The turn: the specific insight, practice, or reframe (1–2 lines)
3. After: the changed state — concrete, not abstract (2–3 lines)
4. Bridge: "wrote 2,000 words on this — link in first comment"
**Avoid:** A Before state that's so extreme it feels like a strawman. An After state that's vague or purely aspirational ("you'll feel more confident"). Missing the bridge — Before/After without a pointer to depth is just a tweet.

### Structure: Provocation to Resolution
**Shape:** Ask the uncomfortable question that the reader has been avoiding → stay with the discomfort long enough to establish stakes → provide a partial but genuine answer → make clear the full resolution lives in the linked piece.
**Best for:** Driving traffic to a longer Substack post or article. The LinkedIn post is a teaser, not a standalone argument — the provocation earns the click; the resolution requires the full piece.
**Section sequence:**
1. The uncomfortable question (line 1, under 150 chars)
2. Stakes: why this question matters and why most people avoid it (2–3 lines)
3. Partial answer: enough substance to be credible, not enough to satisfy (2–4 lines)
4. The invitation: "wrote 2,000 words on this — link in first comment"
**Avoid:** Provoking without any resolution hint — the reader needs enough to know you have an answer worth reading. Over-delivering in the post body (if the full answer fits in a LinkedIn post, it doesn't need a full article). Burying the question after a long setup.
